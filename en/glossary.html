Glossary

Attachment figure Bowlby
A person (often a familiar adult) with whom the child forms a specific, enduring emotional bond. Provides a secure base for exploration and a safe haven in distress. In Bowlby’s theory, there can be multiple attachment figures; it is not necessarily the mother. See also Attachment · Separation anxiety.
Secure base Bowlby
A central concept in attachment theory. The attachment figure provides a stable point of reference from which the child can confidently explore the environment, knowing they can return to safety if frightened or distressed. See also Attachment figure · Attachment.
Cuddly toy / security blanket
Common English names for a soft object (blanket, teddy) a child clings to for comfort. See also Transitional object.
Transitional object Winnicott, 1951
The first object invested by the child, bridging parental presence and the experience of separation. Carries sensory traces (smell, texture) that support emotion regulation and a sense of continuity.
Attachment Bowlby, from 1958
A lasting emotional bond between the child and caregivers. Prompt and sensitive responses secure exploration and reduce separation anxiety.
Separation anxiety
Distress reaction when the child is away from the attachment figure. A cuddly toy can soften its intensity, allowing the brain to maintain vital balance without stress overload.
Potential space Winnicott
Intermediate area between inner world and external reality, where play, creativity, and symbolization emerge. The transitional object “lives” in this space.
Sclerosed Neural Constellations (SNC)
Rigid, compartmentalized neural networks formed when the brain faces experiences it cannot fully integrate (separations, chronic stress, lack of movement, early trauma, etc.). This protective mechanism reduces immediate anxiety but lowers the brain’s plasticity and efficiency. Over time, combined with the massive synaptic pruning of adolescence, these constellations may weaken experiential integration and contribute to certain forms of neural degeneration during aging.
Neural nebulae
Loosely integrated clusters of neurons that may evolve into SNC if interaction diversity remains limited.
Safe haven Bowlby
A concept complementary to the secure base. The attachment figure is experienced as a source of comfort and protection in times of fear, tiredness, or distress. The child turns to this figure to regain calm and emotional safety. See also Attachment figure.
Synaptic pruning
Natural reduction of neural connections—especially strong in adolescence—that increases efficiency, often at the cost of redundancy.
Brain plasticity
The brain’s ability to change with experience. Enhanced by rich, varied, and secure interactions.
Emotion regulation
Mechanisms (bodily, relational, cognitive) that keep arousal at levels compatible with well‑being and learning.
Expressive motor activity
Body movements (posture, gestures, rhythms) that convey internal states and support parent‑child co‑regulation.
Anaclitic depression Spitz
A state of withdrawal and loss of milestones observed in infants separated for long periods from their mother—showing the vital importance of relational continuity.
